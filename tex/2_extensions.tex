\section{Motivation}
ItÃ´'s calculus, even though being very useful, cannot handle anticipating integrands. We motivate this issue with a few examples.

\begin{example}
    For \( t âˆˆ [0, 1] \), consider

        \end{aligned}
        \right.
    \end{equation*}
    This is a stochastic differential equation with \emph{anticipating} initial condition. \end{example}

\begin{example}
    Instead of the initial condition being anticipating, we can also consider the diffusion coefficient to be anticipating.
    \begin{equation*}
        \left\{
        \begin{aligned}
            \dif Z(t)  & =  W_1 Z(t) \dif W_t  \\
                Z(0)  & =  1 .
        \end{aligned}
        \right.
    \end{equation*}
    We run into the exact same issue in this case.
\end{example}

We face the same problem when trying to define iterated ItÃ´ integrals. For example, note that
\begin{equation*}  \label{eqn:double_integral_1}
    âˆ«_0^1 âˆ«_0^1 1 \dif W_v \dif W_u = âˆ«_0^1 W_1 \dif W_u ,

We know that the process \( W_t^2 \) is not a martingale. On the other hand, \( W_t^2 - t \) is a martingale. Can we modify the definition of the integral so that we get this martingale as the result? For deterministic integrands, the answer is yes. We detail this below.

Under \( L^2(Î©) \)-limits, we expect that
\[ X_t = \lim_{n â†’ âˆ} âˆ‘_{i = 1}^n âˆ‘_{j = 1}^n 1 \Del W_j \Del W_i . \]

where the first integral is within ItÃ´'s theory (see \cref{eg:integral_Wt}).

There are some key takeaways from the above computation.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item  We explicitly used the symmetry of the integrand to convert the inside integral into an adapted process.
    \item  The symmetrization introduced a multiplicative factor of \( 2! \).
    \item  The off-diagonal terms gave rise to an ItÃ´ integral, which is a martingale and is exactly what we want the result of our iterated integral to be.
    \item  The diagonal terms gave rise to a function that kills the martingale nature of the ItÃ´ integral. This is due to the quadratic variation of Wiener processes.
\end{enumerate}
